How to Make Telehealth Work (and When You Should See Your Doctor)

In recent years, telehealth has become an increasingly popular way to access healthcare. The COVID-19 pandemic, in particular, accelerated its adoption as patients and doctors alike sought safer ways to manage healthcare. While telehealth offers numerous benefits, it’s not a one-size-fits-all solution. Some situations require a visit to your primary doctor in person. In this article, we’ll explore the best uses for telehealth, common reasons people might dislike it, and when you should opt for an in-person appointment.

What is Telehealth?

Telehealth refers to healthcare services provided remotely via technology, such as video calls, phone calls, or even text messaging. It allows patients to communicate with healthcare providers without having to visit the clinic. Common platforms for telehealth include healthcare apps or dedicated patient portals.

While telehealth is convenient and efficient, it's important to understand its strengths and limitations to ensure that you're receiving the best care possible.

Best Uses for Telehealth

Telehealth is highly effective for many types of care, especially those that don't require a physical examination. Here are some of the best ways to use telehealth:

1. Follow-Up Appointments

If you’ve already been diagnosed with a condition, follow-up visits can often be handled via telehealth. This could include adjusting medications, reviewing lab results, or discussing your progress with treatments. There’s no need to go into the office if the doctor doesn’t need to perform a physical exam.

2. Mental Health Services

Telehealth is excellent for providing mental health counseling and therapy. Many patients find that attending therapy sessions from the comfort of their own home makes it easier to open up and discuss sensitive topics. Teletherapy has been shown to be just as effective as in-person therapy for many patients.

3. Prescription Refills

If you need a prescription refill and there are no changes to your condition, a telehealth appointment can usually handle this. Doctors can assess your need for refills, adjust dosages if necessary, and send prescriptions directly to your pharmacy.

4. Minor Illnesses

If you're dealing with something like a cold, minor skin rash, or mild infections, telehealth is a great option. Providers can assess your symptoms, recommend over-the-counter treatments, and issue prescriptions when needed.

5. Chronic Disease Management

If you have a chronic condition, such as diabetes, hypertension, or asthma, regular telehealth check-ins can help you manage your symptoms without needing to travel to the doctor’s office. Healthcare providers can monitor your condition, review your data, and make adjustments to your care plan remotely.

When to See Your Doctor in Person

While telehealth offers many conveniences, there are times when an in-person visit to your primary doctor is necessary. Physical examinations and diagnostic procedures still require face-to-face interaction. Here are some scenarios where you should consider an in-person visit:

1. Annual Physical Exams

Routine physical exams require a healthcare provider to listen to your heart, check your blood pressure, and conduct other hands-on assessments that simply can’t be done over video or phone. This is especially important if you're managing chronic conditions that require regular monitoring.

2. Severe or Sudden Symptoms

If you're experiencing severe symptoms, such as chest pain, difficulty breathing, or sudden changes in vision, it’s critical to seek in-person care immediately. These types of symptoms could indicate life-threatening conditions like heart attack or stroke, and they require immediate attention.

3. Injuries

If you've suffered a serious injury—such as a fracture, deep cut, or burn—seeing your doctor or visiting an urgent care clinic in person is essential. Telehealth won’t allow a provider to properly assess the extent of the damage or provide the necessary hands-on treatment.

4. Specialized Diagnostic Testing

If you need lab work, imaging tests (such as X-rays or MRIs), or other specialized procedures, you’ll need to see your doctor in person. While telehealth can be used to review results, the tests themselves must be done at a medical facility.

5. Vaccinations

Routine vaccinations, such as flu shots or COVID-19 boosters, require an in-person visit. It's important to stay up to date with your vaccinations, especially if you’re at higher risk for illness.

Common Reasons People Dislike Telehealth

Despite its benefits, some people have reservations about telehealth. Here are a few common concerns, and how to address them:

1. Lack of Personal Connection

Some patients feel that telehealth lacks the personal touch of in-person visits. They may feel less connected to their doctor when communicating over a screen or phone. However, many healthcare providers are trained to offer compassionate care in telehealth settings, and video calls can still facilitate meaningful interactions.

2. Technical Difficulties

Technology issues—like poor internet connection or difficulties using the telehealth platform—can make the experience frustrating. To minimize these issues, practice using the platform before your appointment, and ensure that you have a reliable internet connection.

3. Concerns About Privacy

Some patients worry about the security of their personal health information when using telehealth. It’s important to know that telehealth providers use secure, encrypted platforms to protect your information. Be sure to ask your provider about the security measures they have in place.

4. Limited Physical Examinations

Some people feel uneasy about not having a doctor physically present to assess their condition. It’s true that telehealth isn’t suitable for every situation, which is why it’s essential to recognize when an in-person visit is necessary.

How to Make the Most of Telehealth

Here are a few tips to help you get the most out of your telehealth visits:

• Prepare in advance: Write down any symptoms, questions, or concerns you have before the appointment. Having a list can help you stay focused and ensure you cover everything during the session.

• Choose a quiet, private location: Find a space where you won’t be disturbed, and where you feel comfortable discussing personal health information.

• Test your technology: Make sure your device, internet connection, and telehealth platform are working properly before the appointment.

Telehealth has proven to be a valuable tool in today’s healthcare landscape, offering convenience and accessibility for many medical needs. However, it’s not a replacement for all types of care. Knowing when to use telehealth and when to visit your doctor in person ensures that you receive the right level of care for your situation.

By understanding the strengths and limitations of telehealth, you can take control of your healthcare experience and use this technology to its fullest potential while ensuring your medical needs are met effectively.
