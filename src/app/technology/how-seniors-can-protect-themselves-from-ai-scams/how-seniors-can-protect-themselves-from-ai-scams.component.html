How Seniors Can Protect Themselves from AI Scams

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a technology that can process and analyze large amounts of data very quickly. AI powers tools like voice assistants (such as Siri or Alexa) and can learn patterns from the information it gathers. While AI has made life easier in many ways, scammers are also using this technology to trick people, including seniors. As scammers become more advanced in their methods, it's important for seniors to understand how AI can be used in scams, and how they can protect themselves.

What is AI?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to the ability of computers or machines to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. This includes things like recognizing faces, understanding language, or making decisions based on large amounts of data. Many of the technologies we use daily are powered by AI, such as smartphone apps, online shopping platforms, and even some customer service bots.

However, while AI can help make everyday life easier, it can also be misused by criminals to create scams that are harder to recognize.

Common Types of AI-Powered Scams

AI allows scammers to target people in more sophisticated and personalized ways. Here are some of the scams seniors should watch out for:

1. Voice Cloning Scams

One of the newest threats involves voice cloning, where AI can mimic the voice of a loved one, such as a grandchild or child. Scammers can use just a short audio clip of someone’s voice (which might be found on social media or elsewhere online) to create a realistic voice message that sounds exactly like that person. They might then call you pretending to be that family member, claiming they are in trouble and need money urgently.

• Warning Sign: If you receive a call from a family member asking for money, especially if it seems out of the ordinary, be cautious.

• What You Can Do: Before sending any money, hang up and call the person directly on a number you know is theirs. Do not rely on the phone number provided by the caller.

2. AI-Generated Emails and Messages

AI can be used to create fake emails, texts, or social media messages that appear to come from legitimate sources, such as your bank, a charity, or even a government agency. These messages often ask you to click on a link or provide personal information, such as your Social Security number or bank account details.

• Warning Sign: Be suspicious of unsolicited messages that ask for personal information or offer deals that seem too good to be true.

• What You Can Do: If you receive an email or message asking for sensitive information, don’t click any links. Instead, contact the company directly through their official website or customer service number to verify the request.

3. Deepfake Videos

AI can also be used to create deepfake videos—videos that are manipulated to make it look like someone is saying or doing something they never did. Scammers might use deepfake videos to impersonate public figures or loved ones, asking you for money or personal information.

• Warning Sign: Be wary of any video that looks suspicious or seems out of character for the person in the video.

• What You Can Do: If you suspect a video might be fake, avoid sharing it and verify the source before taking any action.

Sex and Extortion Scams Using AI

Some of the most harmful scams involve sex scams and sex extortion scams, where scammers use AI to exploit victims emotionally and financially.

1. AI-Powered Sex Scams

In sex scams, scammers might use AI-generated images or videos of someone that look real, but are entirely fake. They may use these images to lure victims into inappropriate conversations or trick them into sending money. They could also pretend to form a romantic relationship with you online, asking for gifts or money over time.

• Warning Sign: Be suspicious if someone online is pressuring you into an intimate conversation or asks for money. Scammers often try to create a sense of urgency or a false emotional connection.

• What You Can Do: Never share personal or intimate information with someone you’ve met online, and don’t send money to someone you’ve never met in person.

2. Sex Extortion Scams

In sex extortion scams, also known as "sextortion," scammers may claim to have intimate or inappropriate photos or videos of you. They’ll threaten to release these images or videos unless you pay them a certain amount of money. Sometimes, they use AI to create fake images, making it seem as though they have compromising material, even if they don’t.

• Warning Sign: If someone threatens to release sensitive images of you unless you send money, this is likely a scam.

• What You Can Do: Do not give in to the demands. Report the scam to local authorities or a trusted organization like the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).

How Seniors Can Protect Themselves from AI Scams

While these AI-powered scams are sophisticated, there are several steps seniors can take to protect themselves:

1. Be Skeptical of Unsolicited Communications

Whether it’s a phone call, email, or social media message, always be cautious of unexpected communication that asks for personal information or money. Take a moment to verify the source before responding.

2. Verify Requests for Money

If someone (even if it sounds like a family member) asks for money urgently, always double-check with that person through a separate phone number or in person. Scammers rely on creating a sense of urgency to trick victims into sending money without thinking.

3. Limit What You Share Online

Be careful about what you share on social media platforms. Scammers can use personal information from your public profiles, such as names of family members or your voice from videos, to make their scams more convincing.

4. Report Suspicious Activity

If you receive a suspicious message or encounter someone threatening you with a scam, report it to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) or your local authorities. The AARP Fraud Watch Network is also a valuable resource for staying informed about the latest scams.

As AI technology continues to evolve, scammers are finding new ways to exploit it. However, with a basic understanding of how AI works and a cautious approach, seniors can protect themselves from these sophisticated scams. By staying vigilant, verifying communications, and being cautious about personal information, you can keep yourself safe from these AI-powered threats. If in doubt, always reach out to trusted friends, family, or official resources like the FTC to help you navigate suspicious situations.
