Trauma 101: What Is It and How Do I Manage It?

Childhood trauma is a complex and sensitive subject that can have a profound impact on our emotional, psychological, and physical well-being. Trauma, especially in early life, can shape how we view the world, form relationships, and manage stress. The lasting effects of trauma are not just emotional—they can affect the body in powerful ways, too. This concept is central to the work of experts like Bessel van der Kolk, author of The Body Keeps the Score, and Gabor Maté, known for his research on childhood trauma and addiction.

Understanding what trauma is and learning how to manage it are essential steps in healing and reclaiming a sense of peace in your life. Let’s break down the basic concepts of trauma, how it affects the body and mind, and what steps you can take to manage its effects.

What Is Childhood Trauma?

Childhood trauma can be defined as any experience during childhood that overwhelms a child’s ability to cope with stress. This could include physical or emotional abuse, neglect, or witnessing domestic violence. These experiences can deeply affect a child’s developing brain, influencing their ability to regulate emotions, form secure attachments, and build trust in others.

Bessel van der Kolk emphasizes in The Body Keeps the Score that trauma is not just about what happens to you, but about how your body responds to these events. When trauma occurs, especially in childhood, the body and mind go into survival mode. The brain and nervous system can become “stuck” in this state, even long after the traumatic event has passed. Over time, unresolved trauma can manifest as anxiety, depression, chronic pain, or other mental and physical health issues.

Similarly, Gabor Maté highlights that early trauma can shape lifelong behavioral patterns. He explains how many addictions and mental health struggles later in life can be traced back to early childhood experiences, where emotional needs were unmet or where there was a disruption in attachment. The effects of trauma don’t just disappear as we grow up—they follow us into adulthood unless they are consciously addressed.

How Does Trauma Affect the Body?

The body and mind are deeply connected, and trauma affects both in significant ways. According to van der Kolk, trauma can leave lasting imprints on the brain and nervous system, and the body essentially “remembers” the traumatic experience, even if we consciously try to forget it. This is why trauma survivors often feel unsafe in their bodies, experience physical tension, or have heightened stress responses in seemingly non-threatening situations.

The fight-or-flight response is a natural survival mechanism that helps us respond to danger. However, in trauma survivors, this response can become overactive. When the body is stuck in a state of high alert, it releases stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline, which can lead to chronic stress, fatigue, and even health problems like high blood pressure or autoimmune disorders.

Steps to Manage Trauma

The good news is that trauma can be managed and healed, but it takes patience, self-awareness, and professional support. Below are some steps you can take to begin managing trauma and working toward healing.

1. Acknowledge and Accept Your Feelings

A fundamental step in healing from trauma is to acknowledge that it exists. This might sound simple, but many people try to suppress their feelings or downplay their trauma. Give yourself permission to feel your emotions without judgment, whether it’s sadness, anger, or fear.

Practice naming your emotions. When you feel overwhelmed, take a moment to ask yourself, “What am I feeling right now?” Identifying your feelings can help you regain control and take the first step toward healing.

2. Learn Grounding Techniques

Grounding techniques can help bring your mind back to the present moment when you feel overwhelmed by trauma-related thoughts or feelings. These techniques focus on reconnecting you with your body and the world around you, helping you feel safe in the present.

Try the 5-4-3-2-1 technique: Identify 5 things you can see, 4 things you can touch, 3 things you can hear, 2 things you can smell, and 1 thing you can taste. This can help you refocus and calm your nervous system.

3. Move Your Body

Trauma often gets stored in the body, and physical movement can help release some of this tension. Gentle activities like yoga, walking, or stretching can be beneficial. Bessel van der Kolk emphasizes the importance of reconnecting with your body through movement, which can help shift your nervous system from a state of survival to a state of calm.

Incorporate 10-15 minutes of gentle movement into your daily routine, whether it’s yoga, stretching, or simply going for a walk. This can help release pent-up tension and bring a sense of relaxation.

4. Mindfulness and Meditation

Both Bessel van der Kolk and Gabor Maté stress the importance of mindfulness in trauma recovery. Mindfulness practices help you stay present and observe your thoughts and feelings without becoming overwhelmed by them. Over time, mindfulness can help reduce the intensity of trauma-related emotions.

Set aside a few minutes each day to practice mindful breathing. Focus on your breath, and if your mind starts to wander, gently bring it back to the sensation of your breath moving in and out. This practice can help calm the mind and body.

5. Seek Professional Support

Healing from trauma is not something you have to do alone. Trauma-informed therapy, such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR), can provide support and guide you through the healing process. Working with a professional allows you to address the trauma in a safe, structured environment.

Reach out to a licensed therapist who specializes in trauma. Having professional guidance can help you navigate the complexities of your trauma and offer strategies for healing.

Trauma, particularly from childhood, can have a lasting impact on both the mind and body. Healing from trauma is possible, but it requires acknowledging the deep connection between our emotional and physical experiences. By learning to manage trauma with grounding techniques, movement, mindfulness, and professional support, you can begin the journey toward healing and reclaiming your sense of peace and resilience.

Remember, trauma doesn’t define you—it’s part of your story, but with patience and hope, you have the power to write a new chapter.
