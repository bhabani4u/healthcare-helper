How to Maximize Social Security

In planning for your retirement, you need to know what your projected future expenses will be, how you will fund these expenses, and what your timing will be for each program.

Data is critical in all of these considerations. Creating a retirement plan for yourself and your spouse requires that you consider your life expectancy, calculate your Social Security benefits amount in addition to your other forms of income, determine what form your Medicare will take, and what costs you will expect to pay, and finally, calculate all current expenses in order to budget properly.

For most retirees, Social Security Retirement Income is the largest source of retirement income. But Social Security will not tell you which retirement option to take, nor which will provide the greatest payout. You must know in advance before you begin your Social Security retirement in order to make the right financial decision. Important Considerations

Social Security is the primary source of income for most retirees today. With traditional pensions declining and seniors living decades longer than previous generations, we are undergoing an aging in society that we have yet to manage properly both from a health and financial standpoint. One of the most important questions you must ask yourself is, “when should I begin to collect my Social Security Retirement?” Should you wait until you can receive full retirement benefits before collecting? Are you eligible to collect, based on your spouse’s income? Or will your own retirement benefit be higher?

As of {{currentYear}}, the Full Retirement Age is 67 years old for people born in 1960. The Early Retirement Age is 62 years old. The Delayed Retirement Age is 70. There is a dramatic financial difference between taking Social Security at age 62 compared to age 70.

Retirement Ages & Income

Simply put, the earlier you begin collecting your Social Security Retirement, the less money you will collect over the course of your life. The longer you wait to collect Social Security Retirement, the more money you will collect over the course of your life. This assumes you live into your 80’s, which is very likely.

Once you begin collecting Social Security Benefits, the amount you receive is essentially permanent, minus a Cost of Living Allowance each year, and a slight adjustment that may take place if you continue to work after retiring.

There are three Social Security retirement milestones you must consider:

Retirement Age Milestones
Early Retirement Age (ERA)

1/3 of retirees claim their benefits early (at age 62), opting for a permanently lower retirement income. They may decide this after carefully considering their own life span and need for Social Security income as early as possible. As seen below, the permanent reduction in income can be significant if you take Social Security benefits early:

It is possible to retire early while not taking a permanent hit to your social security payments. For those that retire early but continue to receive employment income, they must be subject to an Earnings Test, which will temporarily decrease your Social Security payments until you stop earning an employment income.

Full Retirement Age (FRA)

Full Retirement Age (age 67) is when you are entitled to 100% of your factored retirement income, which you will receive in perpetuity for the rest of your life. Your Full Retirement Income is calculated by looking back throughout your entire working career, taking your 35 highest earning years, and calculating your retirement income from there. For the self-employed take note! If you are depending on your Social Security income, yet you are able to write off most of your business expenses and pay lower income taxes as a result, this will adversely affect your retirement income.

Delayed Retirement Age (DRA)

Delayed Retirement Age is 70 years old. It may be possible to maximize your retirement income by earning Delayed Retirement Credits. For every year after your Full Retirement Age (age 67) until age 70, you can permanently increase your Full Retirement Age income by 8%, for a total income increase of 24% per paycheck.

Retirement Factors To Consider

Your Life Expectancy and your ability to pay your Expenses are the two biggest factors you must consider when determining when to collect.

Life Expectancy

There are many people who live long lives while also managing long term health issues. So while your health itself must be considered, it is your life expectancy that matters most when considering when to collect.

Break-Even Age for Claiming Retirement Benefits
62 vs. 67 Between ages 78 and 79
62 vs. 70 Between ages 79 and 80
67 vs. 70 Between ages 81 and 82

In order to benefit from delaying your Social Security Retirement, you must live into your 80’s. If you do not believe you will be able to live that long, it makes more sense to begin collecting early.

If you retire at age 62, while you will receive a lower retirement benefit, you will have a head start by collecting benefits for five years (or 60 months of benefits) longer than someone who waits until they are age 67 or older to begin collecting.

If you retire at age 67, you will collect 30% more per month than early retirees, but it won’t be until you are age 78 or 79 before the total amount you have collected matches what early retirees have also collected. The same goes for those that delay benefits until age 70. Those that will live through their 80s and 90s will therefore collect significantly more during their lifetime than early retirees.

Expenses

You can continue to work long after you are eligible for Social Security Retirement. But do you want to? Or do you need to?

There are some who have no choice, and due to injury or health problems you must retire from your job. This does not necessarily mean that you must collect your Social Security Retirement however. If you can delay collecting on your Social Security and you expect to live a long life, it pays to delay.

But if you are unable to pay for your expenses without relying on your Social Security income, you are better off taking an early retirement. You can ease some of your financial loss from Early Retirement by continuing to work. However, this will not necessarily increase your Social Security Retirement entitlement.

Surviving Spouse Benefits

A worker may opt to elect their own Social Security retirement earnings, or the earnings of their spouse. Social Security calculates your retirement income by factoring in your 35 most profitable earnings years. If you were self-employed and deducted most of your earnings due to business expenses, you can expect to receive a reduced retirement income compared to a similarly earning W-2 worker. Or perhaps you are unable to work but your spouse can. In both scenarios, it is important to assess what your spouse will earn from his or her Social Security Retirement, and consider which one will provide the greater payout.

You can only elect one Social Security retirement; you are unable to collect on both incomes, and if you opt to collect on a spouse’s income who later dies, the surviving spouse will only receive their deceased spouses reduced benefit.
