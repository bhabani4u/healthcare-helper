How the Brain Works, Part 1

Your brain is an extraordinary organ that controls every function of your body, from breathing and digestion to complex thoughts and emotions. It’s made up of various regions, each with specialized roles. In this first part of our three-part series on brain function, we’ll explore the key structures of the brain—its lobes, the cerebellum, and the brain stem—and how they work together to regulate everything we do. We’ll also take a look at how the brain changes throughout the lifespan, from birth through old age.

The Lobes of the Brain

The human brain is divided into four main lobes, each with distinct functions. These lobes work together to process information, control movement, and govern our thoughts and emotions.

1. Frontal Lobe: The frontal lobe is responsible for complex cognitive tasks like decision-making, problem-solving, and controlling voluntary movement. It also regulates emotions and personality. This lobe is often referred to as the “control panel” of our brain, making it essential for planning and executing behaviors.

Fun Fact: The frontal lobe continues developing well into early adulthood, which is why teenagers may sometimes struggle with decision-making and impulse control.

2. Parietal Lobe: The parietal lobe processes sensory information related to touch, temperature, and pain. It also helps with spatial awareness and navigation. For example, when you pick up an object, the parietal lobe helps you understand the texture, size, and position of the object.

3. Temporal Lobe: The temporal lobe plays a key role in processing auditory information and is essential for understanding language. It also helps with memory formation and emotional responses. When you recognize a familiar face or recall a past event, you can thank your temporal lobe.

4. Occipital Lobe: The occipital lobe is primarily responsible for processing visual information. It interprets data from your eyes, helping you understand shapes, colors, and movement. Every time you read or look at a picture, your occipital lobe is hard at work.

The Cerebellum: Balance and Coordination

Behind the brain's lobes is the cerebellum, which plays a crucial role in balance, posture, and coordination. Although it’s smaller than the brain’s lobes, the cerebellum is densely packed with neurons that help fine-tune motor activities. Whether you're riding a bike, typing on a keyboard, or simply walking, the cerebellum ensures your movements are smooth and coordinated.

Activities that challenge your coordination, such as yoga or dancing, can help keep your cerebellum sharp and improve your balance.

The Brain Stem: The Body's Vital Control Center

At the base of the brain is the brain stem, which connects the brain to the spinal cord. The brain stem is responsible for automatic, life-sustaining functions like breathing, heart rate, and digestion. It’s divided into three parts: the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata. Together, these parts help regulate many of your body’s most basic functions without conscious effort, allowing you to breathe, digest food, and sleep without thinking about it.

Age-Related Changes to the Brain: From Birth to Old Age

The brain undergoes significant changes throughout the lifespan. Here's a brief overview of what happens as we age:

  • Infancy and Early Childhood: During the early years of life, the brain experiences rapid growth. In fact, at birth, a baby’s brain contains almost all the neurons it will ever have, but it’s only about 25% of its adult size. During infancy, the brain forms billions of new connections between neurons, known as synapses. This period of development is crucial for language learning, motor skills, and sensory processing.
  • Adolescence: The teenage years are marked by continued development, especially in the frontal lobe, which is involved in decision-making and emotional regulation. Adolescents often experience increased risk-taking behavior due to the ongoing maturation of the frontal lobe. This is also a period of synaptic pruning, where the brain refines and strengthens important neural connections while eliminating weaker ones.
  • Adulthood: In adulthood, brain development stabilizes, and the focus shifts toward maintaining the function of neural networks. Cognitive abilities like memory, attention, and problem-solving generally remain strong, though they may begin to decline slightly with age. The brain remains highly plastic, meaning it can still form new connections and adapt, especially when challenged by new experiences or learning.
  • Older Age: As we enter older age, changes in the brain become more pronounced. The hippocampus, which is critical for memory, begins to shrink, making it more difficult to recall recent events. The frontal lobe also shrinks, which may affect decision-making and attention span. These changes can lead to slower cognitive processing and memory challenges, but it’s important to remember that not all cognitive decline is inevitable. Research shows that engaging in stimulating activities like reading, solving puzzles, or learning new skills can help keep the brain active.

Regular mental stimulation through activities like reading, learning new languages, or engaging in creative hobbies can help maintain cognitive function as you age.

Your Brain, Your Health

Understanding the different parts of the brain and how they change throughout life is the first step in taking charge of your brain health. By keeping your mind and body active, engaging in mentally stimulating activities, and staying socially connected, you can help support your brain's health through all stages of life.

Incorporate activities into your routine that challenge both your mind and body. Whether it’s learning a new skill, staying active, or maintaining a balanced diet, these small habits can make a big difference in how your brain functions as you age.
