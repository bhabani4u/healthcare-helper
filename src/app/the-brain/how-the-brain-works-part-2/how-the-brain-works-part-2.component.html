How the Brain Works, Part 2: The Prefrontal Cortex

In part one of this series, we explored the major parts of the brain and how they develop throughout life. Now, in part two, we’ll take a deeper look at one of the brain’s most important regions—the prefrontal cortex—and its role in self-control, decision-making, and empathy. We’ll also discuss how this region develops in young people, what challenges they face as a result, and how to strengthen brain functioning as we age.

What is the Prefrontal Cortex?

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is located in the front of the brain, just behind the forehead. It is responsible for what psychologists call “executive functions,” which include planning, problem-solving, self-control, decision-making, and empathy. It is the part of the brain that allows us to think critically, control our impulses, and understand the emotions and perspectives of others.

In essence, the prefrontal cortex helps us regulate our behavior and emotions in social settings and make thoughtful, deliberate decisions. While other areas of the brain deal with more basic functions (like breathing or processing sensory input), the prefrontal cortex is what makes us capable of higher-order thinking.

Development of the Prefrontal Cortex in Adolescence

One of the most important things to know about the prefrontal cortex is that it is one of the last areas of the brain to fully develop. While much of brain development occurs during early childhood, the prefrontal cortex doesn’t reach full maturity until a person is in their mid-twenties.

This delayed development explains some of the challenges that young people face, particularly in adolescence:

1. Difficulty with Self-Control: Since the prefrontal cortex is still developing in teenagers, they may have trouble controlling impulses and making long-term decisions. This is why teens are often more prone to risky behaviors like experimenting with alcohol or engaging in reckless driving—they simply don’t have the fully developed brain structures needed to weigh the potential consequences of their actions as effectively as adults do.

2. Struggles with Empathy: Empathy, the ability to understand and share the feelings of others, is closely tied to the prefrontal cortex. Because this region is still maturing, teenagers may have difficulty putting themselves in someone else’s shoes or considering how their actions might affect others.

3. Emotional Regulation: The prefrontal cortex works closely with the amygdala, the brain’s emotional center. Since the prefrontal cortex helps regulate emotions, young people may find themselves more prone to emotional outbursts or mood swings because their brain hasn’t yet fully developed the ability to keep these emotions in check.

Encourage teens and young adults to engage in mindfulness practices, like meditation or deep breathing. These activities can help them become more aware of their emotions and learn to control their impulses, even while their prefrontal cortex is still developing.

Strengthening the Prefrontal Cortex as We Age

Even though the prefrontal cortex doesn’t fully develop until our mid-twenties, it is still possible to strengthen brain function well into adulthood. Just like physical exercise can strengthen muscles, certain activities can strengthen the connections in your brain, especially in areas like the prefrontal cortex.

Here are a few simple behaviors that can help enhance prefrontal cortex functioning:

1. Challenge Your Brain with New Activities: Neuroplasticity, the brain’s ability to adapt and form new connections, is something that occurs throughout life. Engaging in challenging mental activities can help keep the prefrontal cortex strong and flexible. Try learning a new language, picking up a musical instrument, or engaging in puzzles or games that require strategic thinking.

2. Practice Mindfulness and Meditation: Research has shown that mindfulness meditation can increase the thickness of the prefrontal cortex and improve attention, self-control, and emotional regulation. By practicing mindfulness, you can strengthen the parts of the brain that help you stay focused and calm under pressure.

3. Exercise Regularly: Physical activity is one of the most powerful tools for improving brain health. Regular exercise increases blood flow to the brain, encouraging the growth of new neurons and helping protect existing brain cells. Aerobic activities, like walking, running, or swimming, are particularly beneficial for the prefrontal cortex and overall cognitive function.

4. Get Enough Sleep: Sleep plays a crucial role in brain health, especially when it comes to memory consolidation and decision-making. Lack of sleep can impair prefrontal cortex function, leading to poor judgment and impulse control. Aim for 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night to support optimal brain function.

Age-Related Changes in the Prefrontal Cortex

As we grow older, our prefrontal cortex undergoes natural changes. Some of these changes are related to normal aging, while others may be accelerated by unhealthy lifestyle choices or certain medical conditions.

  • Cognitive Decline: Starting in middle age, many people experience a gradual decline in certain cognitive functions, including memory, attention, and problem-solving. This is largely due to changes in the prefrontal cortex, such as the thinning of the cortex and a reduction in the number of synaptic connections.
  • Slower Decision-Making: Older adults may notice that it takes longer to make decisions or solve problems, as the prefrontal cortex becomes less efficient at processing information.

However, it’s important to note that cognitive decline is not inevitable. With regular mental and physical activity, you can slow down the aging process in the brain and continue to function at a high level well into your later years.

Engage in regular social interaction and stimulating conversations. Socializing challenges your prefrontal cortex to think critically, understand different perspectives, and manage complex emotions.

Your Prefrontal Cortex and You

Understanding the role of the prefrontal cortex in self-control, empathy, and decision-making can help you better navigate life’s challenges—whether you’re a teenager learning to manage emotions or an adult looking to keep your brain sharp. By engaging in activities that stimulate brain growth, practicing mindfulness, and leading a healthy lifestyle, you can continue to strengthen your prefrontal cortex well into old age.

Try incorporating one or more brain-strengthening activities into your daily routine, such as learning a new skill or practicing mindfulness. With consistent effort, you’ll notice improvements in your ability to focus, manage emotions, and make thoughtful decisions.
