Medicare Overview: The ABDs…and Cs?

Understanding the basics of Medicare and how it works.

While the Medicare system is far from perfect and there are many tradeoffs and financial costs which affect seniors on a fixed budget, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) takes a highly engaged approach to protecting seniors, through increasingly stricter rules which limit how insurance companies may spend the billions of dollars in public subsidies they receive, and stronger safeguards to prevent sales agents from scaring and scamming vulnerable seniors.

If you are preparing to enroll into Medicare within the next few years, you will want to read about Planning Your Retirement first before diving into Medicare.

Medicare Parts: A, B, and D

To begin to understand how it all works, you must split Medicare into its three basic parts: Part A (Hospitalization), Part B (Routine Doctor Visits), and Part D (Prescription Drugs). Each of these three parts operate as completely separate Medicare entities with their own rules.

  • Part A (Hospital) deals with inpatient hospitalization. This includes surgeries at a hospital and overnight stays at a hospital. If you get your blood drawn, see a doctor, or get x-rays at the hospital during surgery or due to hospitalization, this cost would also be billed under Part A.
  • If you or your spouse has worked in the US for ten years and paid taxes into the Medicare system, the government subsidizes your cost for Part A.
  • Part B (Medical) will be most commonly used. It consists of routing appointments to see your Primary Care Physician (PCP) and any specialists you may see for serious issues such as orthopedists or cardiologists. If you get your blood drawn or get an x-ray at your doctor’s office, this will be billed under Part B.
  • Part B has a monthly premium you must pay directly to Medicare, and it is mandatory. This premium varies each year. For {{currentYear}}, the Part B premium is $202.90. If you fail to have Part B coverage, or equivalent coverage through an employer plan, Medicare will levy a permanent monthly penalty on your account.
  • Part D (Prescription Drugs) includes not just prescription drugs, but also vaccines, such as the shingles and flu vaccine which a pharmacist may administer to you. It’s important to note that if you receive an injection at your doctor’s office, this would be billed as a Part B Drug.
  • Part D drug plans come directly from private insurance carriers, and is mandatory. If you fail to have Part D coverage, or equivalent coverage through an employer plan, Medicare will levy a permanent monthly penalty on your account.

If you decide to work past the age of 65 and your employer is willing to provide you will healthcare coverage acceptable to Medicare, you are not required to enroll in Medicare immediately. However, if you do not have suitable alternate coverage and you fail to enroll into all required pieces of Medicare, you will face a permanent penalty for going without them. What are My Base Costs with Medicare?
Part A Costs

The government subsidizes your Part A premium of $600 per month, if you or your spouse have worked in the US for ten years and paid taxes into Medicare during that time. If you fall short of the ten-year work requirement, you can still get Part A by paying a premium for the shortfall. Additionally, there are programs that Healthcare Helper can assist you in applying for which may allow you to receive the full subsidy.

Once you have Part A, you will still have a hospital deductible, and you will still be required to pay 20% of your hospital costs. Other plans, such as Medicaid, Medicare Advantage, and Medicare Supplement, can address this 20% cost.

Part B Costs

Part B is mandatory unless you have what Medicare deems Creditable Coverage once you are eligible for Medicare at age 65. As of {{currentYear}}, Part B also has a monthly premium payment of $202.90, which you will pay directly to Medicare. Any other insurance you sign up for may have its own premium outside of the Part B premium. You will be penalized if you do not have Creditable Coverage or you do not enroll into Medicare Part B once you are eligible. Government-sponsored healthcare, such as COBRA or Affordable Care Act coverage are not considered creditable coverage, and you will be penalized if you remain with this coverage once you are Medicare eligible. Additionally, if you are a high-income earner, you may be required to pay more than this amount per year. Please read on about the Income Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA).

If you do not enroll into Part B when required, you will have a permanent monthly penalty levied on your account.

Note that like Part A, you are also subjected to a deductible, and you will be required to pay 20% of your costs for all Part B medical care. Other plans, such as Medicaid, Medicare Advantage, and Medicare Supplement, can address this 20% cost.

Part D Costs

Part D is also subject to IRMAA, is also mandatory, and requires that you enroll in a drug plan offered by a private insurance company or you will have a permanent penalty on your account. Click here to use our Calculation Tool to determine your penalty.

Because Part D is offered by a private insurance company, your monthly premium, your deductible, the prescription drugs they offer, and what you must pay for each prescription varied by drug plan. On top of that, your costs may also vary significantly whether you use your preferred pharmacy, or your drug company’s preferred pharmacy. Calculating your cost for a drug plan ranges widely, so it helps to have an experienced agent who can run your prescriptions through the system and calculate what your estimated total annual costs will be.

IRMAA

Social Security will increase the basic cost of your Medicare based on your earned income from two years ago. You will need to refer to your tax returns to ensure their calculations are accurate. For instance, if your annual income two years ago was higher than $109,000 as an individual tax filer, or $218,000 as a joint tax filer, you will have to pay more each month to Social Security for Part B and Part D.

If you earned less than $109,000 individually OR less than $218,000 filing jointly two years ago, then you would not be subject to IRMAA for this current year. But if your earnings last year exceeded this amount, then you would be subject to IRMAA next year. And if you plan to withdraw a large sum of money in the future, this may place you into the IRMAA category.

What Can I Do About the 20% Medicare Doesn’t Cover?

Your Red, White, and Blue Medicare card is referred to as Original Medicare, and will cover 80% of your hospital and medical costs. Once you have figured out your Base Costs with Medicare, it’s time to figure out what your Expansion Coverage needs to be in order to cover that 20% shortfall.

For example, the going rate for a routine visit with a doctor may cost you $250. 80% of this cost will be covered by Medicare, leaving you with a bill for the remaining 20%, or $50. Now imagine being hospitalized for three days due to a broken leg. Hospitalization is the most expensive part of healthcare. Your hospital bill could be $130,000. 20% of that cost amounts to $26,000 bill which you would be required to pay.

To cover the remaining 20%, you have two primary options, Medicare Advantage (Part C), Medicare Supplement with a Drug Plan, or Medicaid.

Medicare Advantage Plans

Part C (Medicare Advantage) is often the scary part for seniors because there is so much confusion and misinformation about how they work. But they often are effective in handling that 20% cost in a very affordable way.

Medicare Advantage plans generally consist of HMOs and PPOs, and are only offered by private insurance companies. You are not required to enroll in a Medicare Advantage plan. Because of the tremendous amount of profit to be made, sales agents often aggressively target seniors to persuade them into changing their plan in order to earn a commission. If your plan is working for you, you do not need to change plans.

Medicare Advantage Plans generally operate as either HMOs or PPOs. An HMO means you have a select network of doctors you can see, and in return your costs are lower and you may receive added benefits such as routine dental coverage, which Original Medicare does not cover. A PPO means you still have a select network of doctors you may see, but you can go out of that group as long as you are willing to pay a higher charge to see them.

So long as you are a part of a Medicare Advantage Plan, this means a private insurance company is managing your Medicare, and your Original Medicare has been made temporarily inactive as a result. You cannot use both Medicare Advantage and Original Medicare at the same time. When you opt for a Medicare Advantage plan with a private company, CMS, the organization which runs the Medicare Program, will pay the private insurance company each month in order for them to manage your Medicare Advantage plan. In exchange, Medicare relinquishes all responsibility for your care, and places it in the hands of the insurance company you choose.

Typical additional benefits provided by Medicare Advantage plans include a dental plan, allowance for eyeglasses, transportation, hearing aid coverage, a fitness membership, and an allowance for items such as vitamins and toothbrushes, all of which Original Medicare does not cover. Medicare Advantage Plans typically include prescription drug coverage as well, and offers lower pricing than a standalone Part D drug plan.

Note: for information on other types of Medicare Advantage plans such as Payment Fee For Service (PFFS) or Medicare Savings Accounts (MSAs), please refer to Healthcare Helper’s Medical Glossary.

Medicare Supplement Plans (also called Medigap)

Medicare Supplement Plans work directly with Original Medicare. Original Medicare covers 80% of your cost, and Medicare Supplement will cover the remaining 20% (with exceptions). But, this is done at a price. Think of Medicare Supplement somewhat like car insurance. The closer to 65 you are, the lower your medical costs will be which means your supplement carrier will not have to pay as much out of their pocket to cover you. But every year, your Medicare Supplement rates will increase between 4-9%, because the older you get, the more likely you are to need healthcare, so insurance companies balance out this need by increasing your premium.

Medicare Supplement only works with Original Medicare, and you cannot enroll in both a Medicare Advantage plan and a Medicare Supplement plan.

Medicare Supplement is NOT PPO insurance. PPO insurance means you have a network of doctors you can see at a lower cost, but you can also see doctors outside of that network at a higher cost. In Medicare Supplement, there is no network, which means you can literally see anyone you want, anywhere in the country, and your costs will be covered by the plan. For instance, if see an oncologist in Los Angeles but you wanted to get a second opinion with the top oncologist at the Mayo Clinic in Minneapolis, you can do that under Medicare Supplement.

When you turn 65, Medicare Supplement will typically cost around $150 per month. By the time you are 80 years old, this monthly cost will have more than doubled, calculated in today’s dollars.

Medicare Supplement plans do not cover mandatory Part D Prescription Drug Plans. They also do not cover Routine Dental. While you are not required to sign up for a dental plan, you are required to also purchase a standalone Part D drug plan. As you factor in these costs now and decades to come, this will give you a much clearer picture of your costs, and whether you are prepared to pay for them.

Medicare Part D Prescription Drug Plans

With Medicare, you must be enrolled into a Part D Prescription Drug Plan. You can receive your prescription drug coverage through your employer, TRICARE, Medicare Advantage Plans, or a standalone Part D Prescription Drug Plan.

Individuals who sign up for Medicare Supplement coverage must generally enroll into a standalone Medicare Part D Prescription Drug Plan offered by a private insurance company, even if you do not take a single prescription drug. These plans come with a plan premium, and not all plans cover the same prescription drugs.

Each prescription drug plan will have different covered prescriptions, which is all listed in the Formulary. Even within the same insurance company, their formulary will differ by the different plans they offer. When selecting a prescription drug plan, it’s important that you have your agent price out your medications in order to determine what you can expect to pay. Because your medication costs vary significantly from one carrier to another, it’s especially valuable to do this every year if you take a number of expensive or brand name prescription drugs.

Additionally, take note that there are many discount drug programs that exist to help pay for your prescriptions. Some of these discount programs are offered directly by the drug manufacturer. Others work like a coupon which helps you to reduce your costs. But bear in mind that generally, these discount coupons sell your data in order to offer those discounted prices.

How to Compare Plans

With so many half-truths and misinformation within the Medicare world, seniors are at risk of making poor choices that can hurt their health. The lack of understanding is a major flaw of the system. You must have a basis of knowledge for how the system works in order to protect yourself from being taken advantage of. Another limiting factor to understanding the system is that the healthcare industry is actually broken up into 5 industries, and expertise in one field does not convert to expertise in another. For instance, while medical staff constantly deal with insurance each day, only a small portion actually understand how insurance plans operate or differ. Instead, they must rely on a trusted insurance agent to provide correct information to patients.

To understand how to compare plans, you will want to first understand how to prioritize your healthcare. How to Prioritize Your Healthcare page

From there you will understand that, generally, your insurance plan operates as a middleman, receiving public funding from government agencies, and using these funds to provide certain plan benefits and select Medical Networks. Your cost for care is typically the same across similar insurance plans. So, how do you compare plans?

Start with deciding where you would like to receive your care. Is there a specific doctor you want to see? Is there a specific hospital you trust to take care of you in the event you are in critical condition? Once you determine this, then work your way forward. Figure out which medical groups your doctor works with, then get a referral for a trusted agent who can help you pick out the insurance plan that works with your doctor/hospital, and may also include some benefits that will be helpful for you.

With over 100 plan options available for seniors, it’s impossible to figure this out on your own and have confidence that you are picking the right plan. It’s important to have a competent agent whom you trust that understands the market and what your options are. If you need such an agent, please contact me.
