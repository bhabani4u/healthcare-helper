The Shakedown on Sugar

Sugar is everywhere—added to drinks, snacks, and even savory foods. While sugar can give you a quick energy boost, too much of it can cause serious health problems, like obesity, diabetes, and heart disease. The American Heart Association recommends limiting added sugars to no more than 6 teaspoons (25 grams) per day for women and 9 teaspoons (36 grams) per day for men. But many of us far exceed that amount.

What is sugar?

Sugars—also known as saccharides—are the building blocks of carbohydrates. Sugar is made when plants convert the sun’s energy into sugar and store it. This is called photosynthesis.

Sugar comes in two forms: monosaccharides and disaccharides

• Monosaccharides are made up of only one sugar molecule. They are often called “simple sugars.”

• Disaccharides are made up of two simple sugar unites that are chemically attached to one another.

Sugar in foods can be categorized in two ways: naturally occurring or added

• Naturally occurring sugars are found naturally in foods or drinks, like fruits or milk.

• Added sugars are sweeteners that have been added to foods or drinks during processing or preparation.

Why Is Sugar Harmful?

When consumed in excess, sugar can:

1. Increase your risk of chronic diseases: Diets high in sugar are linked to obesity, heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and even certain cancers.

2. Cause spikes and crashes in energy: Sugar offers quick energy, but once it wears off, it can leave you feeling more tired than before.

3. Lead to weight gain: Sugary foods are often high in calories but lack the nutrients your body needs. They also don’t keep you full for long, leading to overeating.

Hidden Sugars in Foods

Sugar sneaks its way into many foods you wouldn’t expect. Be wary of:

• Salad dressings

• Ketchup and sauces

• Bread

• Yogurt

• Granola bars

These hidden sugars can quickly add up, pushing you past the daily recommended limit.

Simple Ways to Reduce Sugar Intake

1. Read labels: Check for added sugars in packaged foods. Ingredients like high fructose corn syrup, cane sugar, and honey are all forms of sugar.

2. Choose whole foods: Eat more fruits, vegetables, and whole grains, which contain natural sugars balanced by fiber, vitamins, and minerals.

3. Limit sugary drinks: Soda, energy drinks, sweetened coffee, and energy drinks are some of the biggest sources of added sugars. Switch to water, herbal teas, or sparkling water with a splash of fruit juice.

4. Gradually cut back: Your taste buds will adjust over time. Start by reducing sugar in coffee or choosing less-sweet desserts.

5. Use alternatives mindfully: Sweeteners like stevia or monk fruit can offer a sugar-free option, but it's still important to limit their use to avoid developing a persistent sweet tooth.

Final Thoughts

It’s not realistic to eliminate all sugar from your diet, nor is it necessary. But being mindful of how much you’re consuming and making healthier swaps can go a long way toward improving your overall health. Start small, make gradual changes, and remember that balance is key. By reducing your sugar intake, you’ll not only support your heart and body, but you’ll also feel more energized throughout the day.

Action Steps:

• Check nutrition labels for hidden sugars.

• Swap sugary drinks for water or tea.

• Gradually reduce sugar in your daily meals.

By taking these steps, you can enjoy the sweet things in life without putting your health at risk!
