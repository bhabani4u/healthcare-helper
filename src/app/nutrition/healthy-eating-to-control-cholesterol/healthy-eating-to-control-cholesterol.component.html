Healthy Eating to Control Cholesterol

Cholesterol plays a crucial role in your health, but too much "bad" cholesterol (LDL) can increase your risk of heart disease. The good news? You can manage cholesterol levels by eating the right foods.

What is cholesterol?

Cholesterol is a waxy, fat-like substance found in the body’s bloodstream and cells. Your body needs some cholesterol to function well. Cholesterol is made by the body and it’s found in some of the foods people eat. Too much cholesterol can build up in the arteries and increase your risk of heart disease.

What affects cholesterol levels?

Your cholesterol levels are affected by many things including:2

• Diet

• Body weight

• Age

• Gender

• Activity levels

• Genes

Food and cholesterol

There are many foods that may affect cholesterol.

Component Effect on Cholesterol Recommendation Sources
Unsaturated Fats Help lower LDL cholesterol when replacing unhealthy fats. Incorporate more unsaturated fats into your diet from sources like plant oils, nuts, and fatty fish. Olive oil, avocados, salmon, walnuts
Saturated Fats Increase "bad" LDL cholesterol levels. Limit intake of foods high in saturated fats. Red meat, butter, full-fat dairy
Trans Fats Raise "bad" LDL cholesterol and lower "good" HDL cholesterol. Avoid foods high in trans fats. Processed foods, fried foods, baked goods
Dietary Cholesterol May raise cholesterol levels in the blood. Limit dietary cholesterol to less than 200 mg per day. Meat, egg yolks, full-fat dairy
Soluble Fiber Helps lower LDL cholesterol by preventing its absorption during digestion. Consume 5-10 grams of soluble fiber per day, which may result in a 5% drop in LDL cholesterol levels. Oats, beans, fruits like apples and oranges
Plant Stanols and Sterols Help block the absorption of cholesterol in the digestive tract, lowering "bad" LDL cholesterol levels. Consume 2 grams per day, which may help reduce LDL cholesterol by 5-15%. Enriched foods, raw plant oils, nuts, seeds, whole grains, legumes

Foods to Limit or Avoid:

• Saturated Fats: Found in red meat, butter, and full-fat dairy. These can raise LDL cholesterol levels.

• Trans Fats: Present in processed foods, baked goods, and fried foods. They increase LDL and lower "good" cholesterol (HDL).

Foods to Embrace:

• Soluble Fiber: Found in oats, beans, and fruits like apples and oranges. Soluble fiber helps reduce LDL cholesterol.

• Healthy Fats: Choose unsaturated fats from sources like olive oil, nuts, and fatty fish (e.g., salmon). These can boost HDL levels and improve heart health.

Actionable Tips:

• Switch to plant-based oils like olive or canola.

• Increase fiber intake by incorporating more fruits, vegetables, and whole grains.

• Limit processed and fried foods.

By making small, mindful changes to your diet, you can positively impact your cholesterol levels and protect your heart. Speak with your healthcare provider for personalized advice based on your health needs.
