Common Mistakes in Creating a Will

When it comes to estate planning, creating a will is one of the most important steps you can take to ensure your assets are distributed according to your wishes. However, there are common misunderstandings about what a will can and cannot do, and many people overlook important aspects of estate planning that go beyond a simple will. This article will explore some of the most frequent mistakes people make when creating a will, as well as other documents you may need to consider, such as living trusts.

Misunderstanding the Purpose of a Will

A will is a legal document that allows you to outline how you want your assets distributed after your death. While it’s a fundamental estate planning tool, it has limitations. One of the most common misconceptions is that a will governs all aspects of your estate. In reality, certain assets, such as those held in retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and jointly owned property, often pass directly to beneficiaries outside of a will through beneficiary designations or survivorship rights.

For example, if your will specifies that your house should go to your children, but the house is jointly owned with your spouse, it will automatically pass to your spouse upon your death. Similarly, funds in retirement accounts like 401(k)s or IRAs will go to the named beneficiaries, regardless of what’s stated in the will.

Key Limitations of a Will

1. A Will Does Not Avoid Probate: A will must go through probate, a legal process that verifies the will’s validity and ensures debts are paid before the assets are distributed. Probate can be time-consuming and expensive. Many people mistakenly believe that simply having a will avoids probate. If your goal is to keep your estate out of probate, other tools such as living trusts may be necessary.

2. Wills Don't Provide for Incapacity: A will only takes effect after you pass away. It does nothing to address what happens if you become incapacitated and unable to manage your own affairs. For this, you’ll need to create a durable power of attorney and advance healthcare directives to ensure your financial and medical decisions can be managed by someone you trust if you’re unable to make decisions yourself.

3. Not Addressing Estate Taxes: Some individuals mistakenly believe that a will is enough to address tax concerns. However, depending on the size of your estate, you may need additional planning strategies to minimize estate taxes. A will does not shield assets from estate taxes; therefore, it’s essential to work with an attorney to explore options like irrevocable trusts if necessary.

When a Living Trust Might Be Necessary

A living trust is a legal document that places your assets into a trust during your lifetime and allows for the management of those assets if you become incapacitated. Upon your death, the trust assets are distributed to beneficiaries according to the terms of the trust, without going through probate.

Here are some instances where a living trust might be preferable to a will:

  • Avoiding Probate: Unlike a will, assets held in a living trust are not subject to probate. This means your beneficiaries can receive their inheritance more quickly and without court involvement.

  • Privacy: A will becomes a public document during probate, meaning anyone can access its contents. A trust, on the other hand, remains private.

  • Managing Complex Assets: If you own multiple properties, a business, or other complex assets, a trust can be an effective way to manage and distribute them without involving the courts.

Common Mistakes in Creating a Will

  • Not Updating Your Will: Life circumstances change, and your will should reflect those changes. Failing to update your will after major life events—such as marriage, divorce, the birth of children, or acquiring new assets—can cause unintended consequences. For example, an outdated will may leave assets to a former spouse or exclude a recently born child.

  • Choosing the Wrong Executor: Your executor is responsible for carrying out the terms of your will. Selecting an executor who is unreliable or unable to handle complex financial matters can lead to delays and disputes. It’s important to choose someone who is both trustworthy and capable of managing the legal and financial responsibilities of the role.

  • Failing to Include Contingent Beneficiaries: Naming contingent beneficiaries (people who will inherit if your primary beneficiaries cannot) is essential in case unforeseen circumstances arise. Without contingent beneficiaries, assets may be distributed in ways you didn’t intend.

How to Properly Prepare a Will

1. Consult with a Professional: While it may be tempting to use DIY will kits, consulting with an estate planning attorney is the best way to ensure your will is legally sound and tailored to your specific needs. They can help address complex issues, such as blended families, business interests, or special needs beneficiaries.

2. Review Beneficiary Designations: Review all beneficiary designations on life insurance policies, retirement accounts, and other assets. Ensure they align with your wishes as outlined in your will.

3. Consider Additional Documents: In addition to a will, you may need other estate planning tools, such as a living trust, durable power of attorney, or healthcare directive. These documents will help manage your affairs if you’re incapacitated and ensure that your assets are distributed efficiently after your death.

Plan Beyond a Will

A will is an essential tool for estate planning, but it’s just one part of a larger strategy. To ensure your wishes are carried out and that your loved ones are protected, it’s important to understand the limitations of a will and explore additional documents, such as living trusts or durable powers of attorney, to complete your estate plan.

Start by reviewing your current estate plan. If you don’t have one, now is the time to begin. Consult with a financial planner or estate attorney to create a comprehensive plan that addresses your assets, family dynamics, and long-term goals.

By understanding the nuances of a will and how it fits into a broader estate planning strategy, you can make informed decisions and avoid the common mistakes that could affect your loved ones after you’re gone.
